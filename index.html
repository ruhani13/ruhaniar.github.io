Does chain-of-thought genuinely help us catch reward hacking?

Behavioral monitors already perform strongly. The open question: does reading a model's reasoning add real detection power over watching its actions alone — and when does that margin appear or vanish?

Through the Algoverse AI Fellowship, I'm investigating this with a mixed-effects logistic regression framework on reasoning-trace datasets (MALT, TRACE), designed to give the question the statistical power it deserves.

Three years as a data scientist at Spectrum, building deep learning systems over petabyte-scale network data — CNNs that caught faults before customers felt them, pipelines that moved oceans of telemetry.

Somewhere between the pipelines and the models, I became more interested in whether we can trust how a model got its answer — not just what it predicts. Kahneman taught us that humans confabulate reasons for their choices all the time. Why would we assume machines don't?

Now: AI alignment research, asking whether a model's chain-of-thought is a window into its reasoning or just a plausible story — and answering with real statistical rigor, not vibes. Next chapter unfolds in Singapore, at NUS.
